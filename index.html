Does sequence alignment recover function, or only ancestry?

We compare three alignment algorithms — global, local, and a seed-and-extend heuristic modeled on BLAST — across three protein classes with a known, layered relationship: a target enzyme, its likely structural ancestor with an unrelated function, and an unrelated negative control.

01

Background

β-lactamases are the enzymes that make bacteria resistant to penicillin-class antibiotics — they cut open the drug's reactive ring before it can disable the cell-wall-building machinery. There are several unrelated families of them. This project focuses on metallo-β-lactamases (class B) — a zinc-dependent group responsible for some of the hardest-to-treat resistant infections (NDM, VIM, IMP), as distinct from the more familiar serine-based enzymes like TEM or SHV. Structural studies place metallo-β-lactamases' likely evolutionary origin in a much older family: tRNase Z enzymes, which trim the 3′ ends of pre-tRNA molecules. Both fold around the same zinc-binding metallo-hydrolase scaffold, but the two proteins do almost nothing alike in the cell.

That split — same fold, different job — is a standing problem for any tool that infers function from sequence similarity. If an alignment algorithm scores metallo-β-lactamase against tRNase Z as "highly similar," it's really only picking up shared ancestry, not shared function. We use a third, functionally and structurally unrelated protein family (bacterial transcription factors) as a negative control: an algorithm that's actually function-aware should separate TFs cleanly from both of the other groups, while still correctly relating — or correctly failing to relate — metallo-β-lactamase to tRNase Z.

Metallo-β-lactamase (target) tRNase Z (structural homolog) Transcription factor (negative control)

β-lactamase and tRNase Z share the same underlying metallo-hydrolase scaffold (illustrated here as the same blob shape) — evidence they share a common ancestor — but carry out unrelated chemistry. The transcription factor control has no such shared scaffold with either. An alignment method that's actually function-aware shouldn't be fooled by shape alone.

02

Related Phenomena: Three Ways Sequence Misleads You

Our experiment tests one specific way biology decouples sequence from function — divergent evolution, where two genes duplicate from a shared ancestor and then drift apart. It's worth being explicit that this is only one of at least three distinct phenomena that break the "similar sequence → similar function" assumption. The other two involve no sequence divergence at all.

Divergent evolution

our project · metallo-β-lactamase / tRNase Z

Two separate genes, duplicated long ago, drift apart in sequence while keeping the ancestral fold. Different genes, different sequences today, unrelated functions — but a shared structural signature if you look closely enough.

Gene sharing

delta-crystallin / argininosuccinate lyase

Often the same gene, doing two jobs depending on where and how much of it gets made. In duck lenses, the metabolic enzyme argininosuccinate lyase is massively overexpressed and repurposed wholesale as a transparent structural protein — no mutation required, just dosage and location.

Moonlighting

phosphoglucose isomerase (PGI)

The single identical protein performs two mechanistically unrelated jobs depending on context. Inside the cell, PGI runs step two of glycolysis. Secreted outside the cell, that exact same molecule acts as a cytokine — independently rediscovered and renamed three times (neuroleukin, autocrine motility factor, maturation factor) before anyone realized it was one protein.

Put together, these three cases suggest sequence-based tools like the ones we're testing can only ever address the first category. A protein could align at 0% identity with another and still be doing an identical job (gene sharing, moonlighting) — or align at high identity with a close relative and still be doing something completely different (our main experiment). Alignment identity is genuinely informative, but it is not, on its own, a function detector.

03

Methods

Sequences for three bacterial species were pulled from UniProt for each of the three protein classes (nine sequences total), then run pairwise through three alignment algorithms, all implemented from scratch and scored with the standard BLOSUM62 substitution matrix.

Needleman–Wunsch

global · dynamic programming

Forces an end-to-end alignment across the full length of both sequences. Sensitive to length mismatches and unrelated flanking regions.

Smith–Waterman

local · dynamic programming

Finds the single best-matching subregion, ignoring the rest. Better suited to comparing a conserved domain shared by otherwise divergent proteins.

Mini-BLAST

seed-and-extend heuristic

Indexes short exact-match "words," then extends each hit until the score drops off (X-drop). Ungapped and fast — the same core idea as real BLAST.

06

Discussion

Our central question was whether alignment algorithms recover function or only ancestry. Two results so far — one confirmed, one still to be filled in — bear on that question in different ways.

Finding 1 — algorithm choice matters even before biology enters the picture

Before comparing across protein classes at all, our three real NDM-1 sequences (from K. pneumoniae, E. coli, and Acinetobacter) already exposed a real limitation. NDM-1 spreads by horizontal gene transfer on a mobile transposon (Tn125), not by normal vertical inheritance — so these three sequences are near-identical copies of the same gene, not three independently-evolved lineages. Needleman-Wunsch scored the K. pneumoniae vs. E. coli pair at only 88% identity, while Smith-Waterman scored the same pair at 98% and mini-BLAST at 100%. The gap wasn't biological — it came from a length mismatch (our K. pneumoniae entry is the mature protein, missing its signal peptide, while the other two are full-length), which global alignment penalizes and local/seed-based methods ignore. The practical lesson: an algorithm's sensitivity to sequence formatting, not just biological divergence, can swing a headline identity score by 10+ percentage points. Any conclusion from our main heatmap needs to control for this before it's trusted.

Finding 2 — does the algorithm separate function, or only ancestry?

With all nine sequences real, the average cross-class identity looks like this:

Algorithm Lactamase vs tRNase Z Lactamase vs TF tRNase Z vs TF
Needleman-Wunsch17.8%16.6%20.6%
Smith-Waterman31.3%33.0%28.1%
Mini-BLAST56.9%66.7%42.9%

This is the second interpretation scenario, not the first: none of the three algorithms scored metallo-β-lactamase against tRNase Z any higher than metallo-β-lactamase against the unrelated transcription factor. Under Smith-Waterman and mini-BLAST, the lactamase actually scored slightly higher against the TF than against its real structural relative. Needleman-Wunsch and Smith-Waterman both sit in the 17–33% range for every comparison — squarely inside the zone Todd, Orengo & Thornton (2001) identified as where sequence identity stops reliably tracking any relationship at all, structural or functional.

Mini-BLAST's numbers need a caveat before they're read at face value: it reports identity over only its single best-matching short segment (as low as 3–6 residues), not the whole protein. The 100% and 83% scores against the Acinetobacter entries are almost certainly short coincidental word matches — real proteins of this length will often share a random 3-residue substring somewhere — not evidence of a genuine relationship. That's a concrete limitation of ungapped, word-based methods: a short, high-confidence-looking match can be entirely spurious with no way to tell from the score alone.

Conclusion: at this level of sequence divergence, standard pairwise alignment — global, local, or seed-based — could not recover the known structural relationship between metallo-β-lactamase and tRNase Z. The relationship is real (established by X-ray crystallography and shared zinc-binding motifs, not sequence alignment — see Kim et al. and Daiyasu et al. in References), but it's invisible at the sequence level with these methods. Recovering it would require structural alignment tools (e.g. DALI, TM-align) that compare 3D fold rather than 1D sequence — which is itself the point: sequence and structure carry different, only partially overlapping information about a protein's history.

Connecting back to Related Phenomena

Whichever way Finding 2 lands, it only speaks to divergent evolution — one of the three mechanisms discussed above. Even a "successful" algorithm here would still be blind to gene sharing (delta-crystallin/ASL) and moonlighting (PGI), where sequence identity is 100% and the function still differs by context. That's a genuine scope limitation of alignment-based tools worth stating plainly, not a flaw in our experiment — it just means "sequence similarity" and "same function" are related by more than one mechanism, and ours tests only one of them.

Limitations

Our dataset is nine sequences across three species — not enough to draw general conclusions about metallo-β-lactamases or tRNase Z as families, only about this specific comparison. The NDM-1 horizontal-transfer effect (Finding 1) means our "three species" for the lactamase row aren't a clean evolutionary comparison the way the tRNase Z and TF rows should be, which is itself worth flagging rather than hiding.
