Does sequence alignment recover function, or only ancestry?

A real antibiotic-resistance enzyme, its likely evolutionary relative doing a completely unrelated job, and an unrelated protein thrown in as a control — run through three different alignment algorithms to see if any of them can actually tell the difference between "related" and "similar."

Background

Penicillin works by stopping bacteria from building their cell walls. β-lactamases are how bacteria fight back — enzymes that snip open the drug's reactive ring before it ever gets the chance to work. Not all β-lactamases do this the same way, though. We're focused on one specific family here: metallo-β-lactamases, which use zinc ions to do the chemistry, unlike the more familiar serine-based enzymes such as TEM or SHV. That distinction turns out to matter more than it sounds like it should — it's the reason this project exists at all, because metallo-β-lactamases didn't come from nowhere, and the evidence for where they came from is stranger than you'd guess.

In 2018, researchers pulled DNA out of deep-sea sediment — sediment old enough to predate the antibiotic era entirely — and found a gene for an enzyme nobody had characterized before. They named it PNGM-1. When they tested what it actually did in the lab, they found something genuinely strange: it worked as a real metallo-β-lactamase, breaking down antibiotics just fine, but it also worked as a real tRNase Z, trimming pre-tRNA the same way that enzyme family does in every living cell — one folded protein, both activities, both measurable on the lab bench. Their conclusion was that PNGM-1 sits close to the ancestral tRNase Z lineage, and that β-lactamase activity started out as a kind of evolutionary side effect — what biologists call a "promiscuous" secondary function — before later relatives specialized into it completely. tRNase Z came first. Antibiotic resistance, in this specific family, was an accident that stuck.

That's the split our whole experiment hangs on: same fold, wildly different job. If an alignment algorithm scores metallo-β-lactamase against tRNase Z as "highly similar," it's picking up on that shared ancestry — not on shared function, since the two proteins barely overlap in what they actually do. We added a third, genuinely unrelated protein family (bacterial transcription factors) as a control: an algorithm that's actually tracking function should separate TFs cleanly from both of the other groups, while still correctly relating — or correctly failing to relate — metallo-β-lactamase to tRNase Z.

There's a fourth case worth testing, and it's the mirror image of the first one: what happens when two proteins do the same job but don't share ancestry at all? Serine β-lactamases — TEM, SHV, OXA, and relatives — destroy the exact same antibiotics as metallo-β-lactamases, using the exact same overall strategy (breaking the β-lactam ring), but they evolved completely independently, from penicillin-binding proteins rather than the MBL-fold superfamily. Same name, same job, unrelated fold, unrelated history — the textbook case of convergent evolution. If sequence alignment is tracking ancestry rather than function (which is the whole thesis here), it should treat metallo- vs. serine-β-lactamase as basically unrelated, the same way it treats either one against the transcription factor control — despite both being called "β-lactamases."

A sequence aligner that's actually detecting function, rather than ancestry, should score convergent evolution about the same as unrelated — both are historically disconnected from the lactamase in ways that shouldn't produce high sequence identity, whatever their functional overlap. If it instead scored divergent evolution noticeably higher than both of those, that would be the sign it's tracking function rather than ancestry. Our results section shows which of these actually happened.

β-lactamase and tRNase Z share the same underlying metallo-hydrolase scaffold (illustrated here as the same blob shape) — evidence they share a common ancestor — but carry out unrelated chemistry. The transcription factor control has no such shared scaffold with either. An alignment method that's actually function-aware shouldn't be fooled by shape alone.

02

Related Phenomena: Three Ways Sequence Misleads You

Our experiment tests one specific way biology decouples sequence from function — divergent evolution, where two genes duplicate from a shared ancestor and then drift apart. It's worth being explicit that this is only one of at least three distinct phenomena that break the "similar sequence → similar function" assumption. The other two involve no sequence divergence at all.

Divergent evolution

our project · metallo-β-lactamase / tRNase Z

Two separate genes, duplicated long ago, drift apart in sequence while keeping the ancestral fold. Different genes, different sequences today, unrelated functions — but a shared structural signature if you look closely enough.

Gene sharing

delta-crystallin / argininosuccinate lyase

Often the same gene, doing two jobs depending on where and how much of it gets made. In duck lenses, the metabolic enzyme argininosuccinate lyase is massively overexpressed and repurposed wholesale as a transparent structural protein — no mutation required, just dosage and location.

Moonlighting

phosphoglucose isomerase (PGI)

The single identical protein performs two mechanistically unrelated jobs depending on context. Inside the cell, PGI runs step two of glycolysis. Secreted outside the cell, that exact same molecule acts as a cytokine — independently rediscovered and renamed three times (neuroleukin, autocrine motility factor, maturation factor) before anyone realized it was one protein.

Put together, these three cases suggest sequence-based tools like the ones we're testing can only ever address the first category. A protein could align at 0% identity with another and still be doing an identical job (gene sharing, moonlighting) — or align at high identity with a close relative and still be doing something completely different (our main experiment). Alignment identity is genuinely informative, but it is not, on its own, a function detector.

03

Methods

Sequences for three bacterial species were pulled from UniProt for each of four protein classes (twelve sequences total, all real), then run pairwise through three alignment algorithms, all implemented from scratch and scored with the standard BLOSUM62 substitution matrix. The serine β-lactamase entries were chosen to be species-appropriate rather than arbitrary: TEM-1 for E. coli and SHV for K. pneumoniae are the classic, well-characterized enzyme for each species; A. baumannii's is OXA-51-like, its own chromosomally intrinsic serine enzyme.

Needleman–Wunsch

global · dynamic programming

Forces an end-to-end alignment across the full length of both sequences. Sensitive to length mismatches and unrelated flanking regions.

Smith–Waterman

local · dynamic programming

Finds the single best-matching subregion, ignoring the rest. Better suited to comparing a conserved domain shared by otherwise divergent proteins.

Mini-BLAST

seed-and-extend heuristic

Indexes short exact-match "words," then extends each hit until the score drops off (X-drop). Ungapped and fast — the same core idea as real BLAST.

06

Discussion

Our central question was whether alignment algorithms recover function or only ancestry. Two results ended up bearing on that, and they point in slightly different directions.

Finding 1 — algorithm choice matters even before biology enters the picture

Before we even got to comparing across protein classes, our three real NDM-1 sequences (K. pneumoniae, E. coli, Acinetobacter) already caught us off guard. NDM-1 spreads by horizontal gene transfer on a mobile transposon (Tn125), not by normal vertical inheritance — so these three sequences are near-identical copies of the same gene, not three independently-evolved lineages. And yet Needleman-Wunsch scored the K. pneumoniae/E. coli pair at only 88% identity, while Smith-Waterman put the same pair at 98%, and mini-BLAST at 100%. Same two sequences, three very different verdicts. The gap wasn't biological — it came down to a length mismatch (our K. pneumoniae entry is the mature protein, missing its signal peptide, while the other two are full-length), which global alignment punishes and local or seed-based methods simply shrug off. It's a good reminder that an algorithm's sensitivity to how a sequence happens to be formatted can move a headline identity score by ten-plus points, with nothing biological behind it.

Finding 2 — did any algorithm separate function from ancestry?

With all nine sequences real, here's what the average cross-class identity looks like:

Algorithm Lactamase vs tRNase Z Lactamase vs TF tRNase Z vs TF
Needleman-Wunsch17.8%16.6%20.6%
Smith-Waterman31.3%33.0%28.1%
Mini-BLAST56.9%66.7%42.9%

None of the three algorithms scored metallo-β-lactamase against tRNase Z any higher than metallo-β-lactamase against the unrelated transcription factor. If anything, under Smith-Waterman and mini-BLAST, the lactamase scored a little higher against the TF than against its actual structural relative — which is almost the opposite of what we expected going in. Needleman-Wunsch and Smith-Waterman both land in the 17–33% range across the board, which happens to be right where Todd, Orengo & Thornton (2001) found that sequence identity stops reliably tracking any relationship at all, structural or functional.

Mini-BLAST's numbers need a footnote before anyone takes them at face value: it reports identity over just its single best-matching segment, sometimes only 3–6 residues long, not the whole protein. The 100% and 83% scores against the Acinetobacter entries are almost certainly short coincidental word matches — proteins this size will often share a random 3-residue stretch somewhere by chance — not evidence of anything real. That's a genuine limitation of ungapped, word-based methods: a short match can look extremely confident and still be meaningless, and there's no way to tell the difference from the score alone.

Put plainly: at this level of divergence, none of the three approaches — global, local, or seed-based — could recover the known relationship between metallo-β-lactamase and tRNase Z. That relationship is real; it's established by X-ray crystallography and the enzymes' shared zinc-binding motifs (see Lee et al. and Daiyasu et al. in References), and reinforced directly by PNGM-1, an enzyme that still does both jobs today. But it's invisible at the sequence level to every method we tried. Actually recovering it would take structural alignment tools that compare 3D shape instead of 1D sequence — which is really the whole point. Sequence and structure carry overlapping but genuinely different information about a protein's history, and ours only had access to one of them.

Finding 3 — the mirror test: convergent evolution

Finding 2 asked whether shared ancestry with different function gets mistaken for similarity. The natural follow-up is the reverse case: does shared function with no shared ancestry — serine β-lactamases, which destroy the same antibiotics through a completely independent evolutionary origin — get mistaken for similarity too? If alignment is really tracking ancestry rather than function, it shouldn't. Here's what all three real serine-lactamase sequences (TEM-1, SHV, and OXA-51-like, one per species) actually showed, averaged against the metallo-lactamase target:

Algorithm vs Serine-lactamase (convergent) vs tRNase Z (divergent) vs TF (unrelated)
Needleman-Wunsch16.9%17.8%16.6%
Smith-Waterman32.2%31.3%33.0%
Mini-BLAST57.1%56.9%66.7%

All three species, all real UniProt sequences — no placeholders in this comparison.

This is a clean result, and it's the confirmatory half of the project: the metallo/serine-lactamase pair — despite sharing a name and destroying the same antibiotics — scores essentially the same as the metallo-lactamase/TF pair, across all three algorithms, and lands right alongside the divergent-evolution numbers from Finding 2 too. All three comparisons cluster in the same range per algorithm. Sharing a function without sharing ancestry produced no more sequence similarity than sharing nothing at all — and, just as tellingly, sharing ancestry without sharing function didn't stand out either. Combined, the picture is now symmetric: these algorithms track sequence descent, not biological role, in both directions we tested it.

Connecting back to Related Phenomena

Between Finding 2 and Finding 3, we've now tested two of the four cells in the grid above — divergent evolution and convergent evolution — and both point the same direction. But that still leaves gene sharing (delta-crystallin/ASL) and moonlighting (PGI) completely untouched, and no algorithm we tried would have any chance with either: both involve 100% sequence identity — literally the same protein — with function changing anyway. That's a real scope limitation of alignment-based tools, and it's worth saying outright rather than quietly stepping around it: "sequence similarity" and "same function" are connected by more than one mechanism, and we've only tested two of the (at least) four ways they can come apart.

Limitations

Our dataset is twelve sequences across three species and four protein classes — not enough to draw general conclusions about any of these protein families, only about this specific comparison. The NDM-1 horizontal-transfer effect (Finding 1) means our "three species" for the lactamase row aren't a clean evolutionary comparison the way the tRNase Z, serine-lactamase, and TF rows should be. All twelve sequences are now real — the one small gap left is a citation detail, not a data problem: the A. baumannii tRNase Z entry is missing its UniProt accession number (see References), which doesn't affect any result but is worth closing before final submission.

07

Where the Human Still Has to Show Up

There's a tidy version of this project's conclusion that just says "the tool has a blind spot — use a better tool." We don't think that's quite right, or at least it isn't the most useful way to end this.

PNGM-1 wasn't found by an algorithm scanning for matches. It was found because a group of researchers thought to look somewhere nobody had looked — deep-sea sediment nobody expected an antibiotic-resistance gene to be hiding in — and then noticed something in their lab results that didn't fit the existing story, and followed that thread instead of writing it off as noise. No sequence aligner would have flagged PNGM-1 as interesting; on paper it looks unremarkable, another obscure metallo-hydrolase. What actually made it the missing link was a person asking "wait, why is this one enzyme doing two completely different jobs?" and taking that question seriously enough to test it at the bench.

Our own experiment makes a version of the same point, from the other direction. Every algorithm we built, run the way real bioinformatics tools actually get used, failed to notice a real evolutionary relationship that structural biologists have known about for years. That's not really an argument for reaching for a fancier algorithm instead — it's an argument for not fully trusting a tool's silence. If we'd stopped at "the heatmap says these aren't related," we'd have missed the entire reason this project exists.

We built this with a lot of computational help along the way, including from an AI assistant, and we think that's worth saying plainly instead of glossing over. That help was genuinely useful — for writing alignment code, tracking down real sequences, catching an early mistake before it went further. But at every point this project got somewhere actually interesting, it was because a person noticed something a tool by itself wouldn't have flagged: a wrong assumption worth questioning, a strange result worth double-checking, a question worth asking that nothing automated would have thought to ask on its own. Tools are how you move fast. They're not how you know what's actually worth looking at, or when to stop trusting a number just because it came out of a program. That part still has to be us — and given how much science still depends on exactly that kind of noticing, we don't think that's going away anytime soon.
